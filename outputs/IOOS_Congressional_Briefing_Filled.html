IOOS Economic Impact

The Forecast Behind the Forecast: Ocean Observations Power America’s Coastal Economy

Prepared for: Congressional Staff June 30, 2026

America’s coastal economy depends on forecasts that start in the water. IOOS and NOAA observing systems turn water-level, current, biological, chemical, and storm data into operational decisions for ports, shellfish managers, emergency responders, hatcheries, and coastal planners. How IOOS Creates Economic Value

IOOS converts sustained ocean observations into trusted data products, forecasts, and decisions that keep coastal commerce and public safety systems moving.

  • Observations: PORTS® water levels and currents, HF radar surface currents, gliders, ocean acidification stations, HAB observations, and long-term coastal water-level stations.
  • Data Integration: IOOS regional associations and NOAA systems standardize, quality-control, and distribute observations for operational users.
  • Models & Forecasts: HAB forecasts, SAROPS drift prediction, hurricane intensity models, inundation dashboards, and port decision-support tools depend on these inputs.
  • Decisions: Pilots, port operators, shellfish managers, hatcheries, the Coast Guard, emergency managers, and planners act on the resulting information.
  • Economic Outcomes: The matrix documents avoided costs, loading efficiency, targeted closures, search efficiency, preparedness, business revenue, and time savings where sources support those claims.

Risk of Underinvestment

Underinvestment erodes the observing, integration, and forecast chain before users see it: fewer trusted inputs, less reliable decision support, and weaker evidence for economic returns.

  • Ports and pilots lose real-time water-level, current, meteorological, and air-gap information that supports safe navigation and loading decisions.
  • Shellfish managers and hatcheries lose forecast and early-warning data used for targeted monitoring, closures, intake timing, and production scheduling.
  • USCG planners, hurricane forecasters, emergency managers, and coastal planners lose surface-current, glider, and water-level observations that support search planning, intensity forecasts, and flood response.
THE ASK
Sustain and strengthen NOAA IOOS observing, regional data integration, and decision-support services, and direct NOAA to update economic valuation for PORTS®, HF radar, HAB forecasts, ocean acidification monitoring, hurricane gliders, and coastal hazards products so appropriators have current benefit estimates.
